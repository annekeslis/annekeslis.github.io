Producing speech is one of the most complex motor skills humans develop, navigating between linguistic requirements on the one side and sensory and articulatory constraints on the other side. One striking feature observed during speech production is articulatory variability — the nature of articulatory movement behavior  and degree of variability to realize linguistic targets depends on context, dialect, skill, or individual physical characteristics. Despite this variability, listeners can still accurately extract the required linguistic information to understand a message. 

My research aims to understand how speakers control their articulators to realize abstract speech targets and to identify the factors that lead to breakdowns in articulation, despite intact planning of speech units. The factors of interest include (cross-) linguistic complexity, different types of motor speech disorders, such as dysarthria, availability of sensory feedback in controlling articulatory movements, and all their interactions. 
My work spans three main topics:

## Understanding Articulatory Movement and Variability

Speech is a complex motor activity that requires highly coordinated movements of the tongue, lips, and jaw. My research examines how these articulatory movements are organized in typical speech and how they change in neurodegenerative disorders that cause dysarthria. By combining kinematic methods (tracking physical movements) with acoustic analyses, this work aims to uncover how speech motor control functions—and why it sometimes breaks down.

## Differentiating Types of Dysarthria

Dysarthria is not a single disorder but a family of speech impairments caused by different neurological conditions. A key goal of my research is to identify the articulatory and acoustic signatures that distinguish these subtypes. By comparing speech from individuals with different forms of dysarthria and neurobiologically healthy speakers across multiple languages, this work contributes to a more precise understanding of these disorders and supports the development of better diagnostic tools and targeted therapies.

## Bridging Clinical and Theoretical Perspectives

Another focus of my research explores how other motor speech disorders—such as developmental stuttering—relate to dysarthria. In particular, I investigate how fluency-enhancing techniques like metronome pacing and noise masking influence articulatory behavior in people who stutter and in individuals with Parkinson’s disease. These studies help clarify both the shared and distinct mechanisms underlying different motor speech disorders.
